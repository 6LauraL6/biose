</tbody></table><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Chemical compound</div>
<p><b>Thymine</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/θ/: &#39;th&#39; in &#39;thigh&#39;">θ</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) (<a href="/wiki/Nucleoside#List_of_nucleosides_and_corresponding_nucleobases" title="Nucleoside">symbol</a> <b>T</b> or <b>Thy</b>) is one of the four <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">nucleobases</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acid</a> of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> that are represented by the letters G–C–A–T. The others are <a href="/wiki/Adenine" title="Adenine">adenine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a>.  Thymine is also known as <b>5-methyluracil,</b> a <a href="/wiki/Pyrimidine" title="Pyrimidine">pyrimidine</a> nucleobase. In <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, thymine is replaced by the nucleobase <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a>.  Thymine was first isolated in 1893 by <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Kossel" title="Albrecht Kossel">Albrecht Kossel</a> and Albert Neumann from calf <a href="/wiki/Thymus" title="Thymus">thymus</a> glands, hence its name.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivation">Derivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thymine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Derivation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As its alternate name (5-methyluracil) suggests, thymine may be derived by <a href="/wiki/Methylation" title="Methylation">methylation</a> of uracil at the 5th carbon. In <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, thymine is replaced with <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a> in most cases.  In DNA, thymine (T) binds to <a href="/wiki/Adenine" title="Adenine">adenine</a> (A) via two hydrogen bonds, thereby stabilizing the nucleic acid structures.
</p><p>Thymine combined with <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a> creates the <a href="/wiki/Nucleoside" title="Nucleoside">nucleoside</a> <a href="/wiki/Deoxythymidine" class="mw-redirect" title="Deoxythymidine">deoxythymidine</a>, which is synonymous with the term <a href="/wiki/Thymidine" title="Thymidine">thymidine</a>. Thymidine can be phosphorylated with up to three phosphoric acid groups, producing <a href="/wiki/DTMP" class="mw-redirect" title="DTMP">dTMP</a> (<b>d</b>eoxy<b>t</b>hymidine <b>m</b>ono<b>p</b>hosphate), <a href="/wiki/DTDP" class="mw-redirect" title="DTDP">dTDP</a>, or <a href="/wiki/DTTP" class="mw-redirect" title="DTTP">dTTP</a> (for the <b>d</b>i- and <b>t</b>ri- phosphates, respectively).
</p><p>One of the common mutations of DNA involves two adjacent thymines or cytosine, which, in presence of <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet light</a>, may form <a href="/wiki/Thymine_dimers" class="mw-redirect" title="Thymine dimers">thymine dimers</a>, causing "kinks" in the DNA molecule that inhibit normal function.
</p><p>Thymine could also be a target for actions of 5-<a href="/wiki/Fluorouracil" title="Fluorouracil">fluorouracil</a> (5-FU) in <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> treatment. 5-FU can be a metabolic analog of thymine (in DNA synthesis) or uracil (in RNA synthesis).  Substitution of this analog inhibits <a href="/wiki/DNA_replication" title="DNA replication">DNA synthesis</a> in actively dividing cells.
</p><p>Thymine bases are frequently oxidized to <a href="/wiki/Hydantoin" title="Hydantoin">hydantoins</a> over time after the death of an organism.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thymine_imbalance_causes_mutation">Thymine imbalance causes mutation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thymine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Thymine imbalance causes mutation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During growth of <a href="/wiki/Escherichia_virus_T4" title="Escherichia virus T4">bacteriophage T4</a>, an imbalance of thymine availability, either a deficiency or an excess of thymine, causes increased <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  The mutations caused by thymine deficiency appear to occur only at AT <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">base pair</a> sites in DNA and are often AT to GC <a href="/wiki/Transition_(genetics)" title="Transition (genetics)">transition mutations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>   In the bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>, thymine deficiency was also found to be mutagenic and cause AT to GC transitions.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_aspects">Theoretical aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thymine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theoretical aspects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2015, NASA scientists reported that, for the first time, complex <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a> and thymine, have been formed in the laboratory under <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> conditions, using starting chemicals, such as <a href="/wiki/Pyrimidine" title="Pyrimidine">pyrimidine</a>, found in <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a>. Pyrimidine, like <a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (PAHs), another carbon-rich compound, may have been formed in <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giants</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">interstellar dust</a> and gas clouds, according to the scientists.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA-20150303_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20150303-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Thymine has not been found in meteorites, which suggests the first strands of DNA had to look elsewhere to obtain this building block. Thymine likely formed within some meteorite parent bodies, but may not have persisted within these bodies due to an <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation</a> reaction with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thymine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synthesis"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>
