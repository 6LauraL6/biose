<p><b>Cytosine</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>,<span class="wrap"> </span>-<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>,<span class="wrap"> </span>-<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>) (<a href="/wiki/Nucleoside#List_of_nucleosides_and_corresponding_nucleobases" title="Nucleoside">symbol</a> <b>C</b> or <b>Cyt</b>) is one of the four <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">nucleobases</a> found in <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Adenine" title="Adenine">adenine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a> in RNA). It is a <a href="/wiki/Pyrimidine" title="Pyrimidine">pyrimidine</a> derivative, with a <a href="/wiki/Heterocyclic" class="mw-redirect" title="Heterocyclic">heterocyclic</a> <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_ring" class="mw-redirect" title="Aromatic ring">aromatic ring</a> and two substituents attached (an <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> group at position 4 and a <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">keto</a> group at position 2). The <a href="/wiki/Nucleoside" title="Nucleoside">nucleoside</a> of cytosine is <a href="/wiki/Cytidine" title="Cytidine">cytidine</a>.  In Watson-Crick <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">base pairing</a>, it forms three <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a> with <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytosine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cytosine was discovered and named by <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Kossel" title="Albrecht Kossel">Albrecht Kossel</a> and Albert Neumann in 1894 when it was hydrolyzed from calf <a href="/wiki/Thymus" title="Thymus">thymus</a> tissues.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A structure was proposed in 1903, and was synthesized (and thus confirmed) in the laboratory in the same year.
</p><p>In 1998, cytosine was used in an early demonstration of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_information_processing" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantum information processing">quantum information processing</a>  when Oxford University researchers implemented the <a href="/wiki/Deutsch%E2%80%93Jozsa_algorithm" title="Deutsch–Jozsa algorithm">Deutsch–Jozsa algorithm</a> on a two <a href="/wiki/Qubit" title="Qubit">qubit</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance_quantum_computer#Overview_of_Liquid_State_Nuclear_Magnetic_Resonance_Quantum_Information_Processing" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance quantum computer">nuclear magnetic resonance quantum computer (NMRQC)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2015, NASA scientists reported the formation of cytosine, along with uracil and thymine, from <a href="/wiki/Pyrimidine" title="Pyrimidine">pyrimidine</a> under the space-like laboratory conditions, which is of interest because pyrimidine has been found in meteorites although its origin is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA-20150303_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20150303-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_reactions">Chemical reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytosine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical reactions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Cytosine can be found as part of DNA, as part of RNA, or as a part of a <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotide</a>. As <a href="/wiki/Cytidine_triphosphate" title="Cytidine triphosphate">cytidine triphosphate</a> (CTP), it can act as a co-factor to enzymes, and can transfer a phosphate to convert <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">adenosine diphosphate</a> (ADP) to <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP).
</p><p>In DNA and RNA, cytosine is paired with <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>. However, it is inherently unstable, and can change into <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a> (<a href="/wiki/Deamination" title="Deamination">spontaneous deamination</a>). This can lead to a <a href="/wiki/Point_mutation" title="Point mutation">point mutation</a> if not repaired by the <a href="/wiki/DNA_repair" title="DNA repair">DNA repair</a> <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> such as uracil glycosylase, which cleaves a uracil in DNA.
</p><p>Cytosine can also be <a href="/wiki/Methylation" title="Methylation">methylated</a> into <a href="/wiki/5-Methylcytosine" title="5-Methylcytosine">5-methylcytosine</a> by an enzyme called <a href="/wiki/DNA_methyltransferase" title="DNA methyltransferase">DNA methyltransferase</a> or be methylated and <a href="/wiki/Hydroxylation" title="Hydroxylation">hydroxylated</a> to make <a href="/wiki/5-Hydroxymethylcytosine" title="5-Hydroxymethylcytosine">5-hydroxymethylcytosine</a>. The difference in rates of deamination of cytosine and 5-methylcytosine (to uracil and <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a>) forms the basis of <a href="/wiki/Bisulfite_sequencing" title="Bisulfite sequencing">bisulfite sequencing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_function">Biological function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytosine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biological function"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When found third in a <a href="/wiki/Codon" class="mw-redirect" title="Codon">codon</a> of <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, cytosine is synonymous with <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a>, as they are interchangeable as the third base.
When found as the second base in a codon, the third is always interchangeable. For example, UCU, UCC, UCA and UCG are all <a href="/wiki/Serine" title="Serine">serine</a>, regardless of the third base.
</p><p>Active enzymatic deamination of cytosine or 5-methylcytosine by the <a href="/wiki/APOBEC" title="APOBEC">APOBEC</a> family of cytosine deaminases could have both beneficial and detrimental implications on various cellular processes as well as on organismal evolution.<sup id="cite_ref-Chahwan_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chahwan-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The implications of deamination on 5-hydroxymethylcytosine, on the other hand, remains less understood.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_aspects">Theoretical aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cytosine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theoretical aspects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until October 2021, Cytosine had not been found in meteorites, which suggested the first strands of RNA and DNA had to look elsewhere to obtain this building block. Cytosine likely formed within some meteorite parent bodies, however did not persist within these bodies due to an effective <a href="/wiki/Deamination" title="Deamination">deamination</a> reaction into <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2021, Cytosine was announced as having been found in meteorites by researchers in a joint Japan/NASA project, that used novel methods of detection which avoided damaging nucleotides as they were extracted from meteorites.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
