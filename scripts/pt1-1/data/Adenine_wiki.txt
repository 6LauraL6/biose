<p><b>Adenine</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) (<a href="/wiki/Nucleoside#List_of_nucleosides_and_corresponding_nucleobases" title="Nucleoside">symbol</a> <b>A</b> or <b>Ade</b>) is a  <a href="/wiki/Purine" title="Purine">purine</a> <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">nucleobase</a>. It is one of the four nucleobases in the <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a> of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, the other three being <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a> (G), <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a> (C), and <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a> (T). Adenine derivatives have various roles in <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> including <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>, in the form of both the energy-rich <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP) and the <a href="/wiki/Cofactor_(biochemistry)" title="Cofactor (biochemistry)">cofactors</a> <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide</a> (NAD), <a href="/wiki/Flavin_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Flavin adenine dinucleotide">flavin adenine dinucleotide</a> (FAD) and <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme_A" title="Coenzyme A">Coenzyme A</a>. It also has functions in <a href="/wiki/Protein_biosynthesis" title="Protein biosynthesis">protein synthesis</a> and as a chemical component of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The shape of adenine is complementary to either <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a> in <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> or <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a> in <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>.
</p><p>The adjacent image shows pure adenine, as an independent molecule. When connected into DNA, a <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bond</a> is formed between <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a> sugar and the bottom left nitrogen (thereby removing the existing hydrogen atom). The remaining structure is called an <i>adenine residue</i>, as part of a larger molecule. <a href="/wiki/Adenosine" title="Adenosine">Adenosine</a> is adenine reacted with <a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">ribose</a>, as used in <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> and ATP; deoxyadenosine is adenine attached to <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a>, as used to form DNA.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adenine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Adenine_numbered.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Adenine_numbered.svg/150px-Adenine_numbered.svg.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="142" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Adenine_numbered.svg/225px-Adenine_numbered.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Adenine_numbered.svg/300px-Adenine_numbered.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="486" /></a><figcaption>Adenine structure, with standard numbering of positions in red.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Adenine forms several <a href="/wiki/Tautomer" title="Tautomer">tautomers</a>, compounds that can be rapidly interconverted and are often considered equivalent. However, in isolated conditions, i.e. in an inert gas matrix and in the gas phase, mainly the 9H-adenine tautomer is found.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biosynthesis">Biosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adenine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biosynthesis"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Purine_metabolism" title="Purine metabolism">Purine metabolism</a> involves the formation of adenine and <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>. Both adenine and guanine are derived from the nucleotide <a href="/wiki/Inosine_monophosphate" class="mw-redirect" title="Inosine monophosphate">inosine monophosphate</a> (IMP), which in turn is synthesized from a pre-existing <a href="/wiki/Ribose_phosphate" class="mw-redirect" title="Ribose phosphate">ribose phosphate</a> through a complex pathway using atoms from the <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glutamine" title="Glutamine">glutamine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aspartic_acid" title="Aspartic acid">aspartic acid</a>, as well as the coenzyme <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofolate" class="mw-redirect" title="Tetrahydrofolate">tetrahydrofolate</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing_method">Manufacturing method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adenine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Manufacturing method"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Patented Aug. 20, 1968, the current recognized method of industrial-scale production of adenine is a modified form of the formamide method. This method heats up <a href="/wiki/Formamide" title="Formamide">formamide</a> under 120 degree Celsius conditions within a sealed flask for 5 hours to form adenine. The reaction is heavily increased  in quantity by using a phosphorus oxychloride (<a href="/wiki/Phosphoryl_chloride" title="Phosphoryl chloride">phosphoryl chloride</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_pentachloride" title="Phosphorus pentachloride">phosphorus pentachloride</a> as an acid catalyst and sunlight or ultraviolet conditions. After the 5 hours have passed and the formamide-phosphorus oxychloride-adenine solution cools down, water is put into the flask containing the formamide and now-formed adenine. The water-formamide-adenine solution is then poured through a filtering column of activated charcoal. The water and formamide molecules, being small molecules, will pass through the charcoal and into the waste flask; the large adenine molecules, however, will attach or "adsorb" to the charcoal due to the <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals forces</a> that interact between the adenine and the carbon in the charcoal. Because charcoal has a large surface area, it's able to capture the majority of molecules that pass a certain size (greater than water and formamide) through it. To extract the adenine from the charcoal-adsorbed adenine, ammonia gas dissolved in water (<a href="/wiki/Ammonia_solution" title="Ammonia solution">aqua ammonia</a>) is poured onto the activated charcoal-adenine structure to liberate the adenine into the ammonia-water solution. The solution containing water, ammonia, and adenine is then left to air dry, with the adenine losing solubility due to the loss of ammonia gas that previously made the solution basic and capable of dissolving adenine, thus causing it to crystallize into a pure white powder that can be stored.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adenine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Function"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adenine is one of the two purine <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">nucleobases</a> (the other being <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>) used in forming <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotides</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>. In DNA, adenine binds to <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a> via two <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a> to assist in stabilizing the nucleic acid structures. In RNA, which is used for <a href="/wiki/Protein_biosynthesis" title="Protein biosynthesis">protein synthesis</a>, adenine binds to <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a>.
</p>

<p>Adenine forms <a href="/wiki/Adenosine" title="Adenosine">adenosine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nucleoside" title="Nucleoside">nucleoside</a>, when attached to <a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">ribose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Deoxyadenosine" title="Deoxyadenosine">deoxyadenosine</a> when attached to <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a>. It forms <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP), a <a href="/wiki/Nucleoside_triphosphate" title="Nucleoside triphosphate">nucleoside triphosphate</a>, when three <a href="/wiki/Phosphate_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Phosphate group">phosphate groups</a> are added to adenosine. Adenosine triphosphate is used in cellular metabolism as one of the basic methods of transferring <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> between <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reactions" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical reactions">chemical reactions</a>. ATP is thus a derivative of adenine, <a href="/wiki/Adenosine" title="Adenosine">adenosine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_adenosine_monophosphate" title="Cyclic adenosine monophosphate">cyclic adenosine monophosphate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">adenosine diphosphate</a>.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adenine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Template_from_Crick_and_Watson%E2%80%99s_DNA_molecular_model,_1953._(9660573227).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Template_from_Crick_and_Watson%E2%80%99s_DNA_molecular_model%2C_1953._%289660573227%29.jpg/220px-Template_from_Crick_and_Watson%E2%80%99s_DNA_molecular_model%2C_1953._%289660573227%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Template_from_Crick_and_Watson%E2%80%99s_DNA_molecular_model%2C_1953._%289660573227%29.jpg/330px-Template_from_Crick_and_Watson%E2%80%99s_DNA_molecular_model%2C_1953._%289660573227%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Template_from_Crick_and_Watson%E2%80%99s_DNA_molecular_model%2C_1953._%289660573227%29.jpg/440px-Template_from_Crick_and_Watson%E2%80%99s_DNA_molecular_model%2C_1953._%289660573227%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1250" data-file-height="971" /></a><figcaption>Adenine on Crick and Watson's DNA molecular model, 1953. The picture is shown upside down compared to most modern drawings of adenine, such as those used in this article.</figcaption></figure>
<p>In older literature, adenine was sometimes called <b>Vitamin B<sub>4</sub></b>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Due to it being synthesized by the body and not essential to be obtained by diet, it does not meet the definition of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamin</a> and is no longer part of the <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B" class="mw-redirect" title="Vitamin B">Vitamin B</a> complex. However, two B vitamins, <a href="/wiki/Niacin_(substance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Niacin (substance)">niacin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Riboflavin" title="Riboflavin">riboflavin</a>, bind with adenine to form the essential cofactors <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide</a> (NAD) and <a href="/wiki/Flavin_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Flavin adenine dinucleotide">flavin adenine dinucleotide</a> (FAD), respectively. <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Emil_Fischer" class="mw-redirect" title="Hermann Emil Fischer">Hermann Emil Fischer</a> was one of the early scientists to study adenine.
</p><p>It was named in 1885 by <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Kossel" title="Albrecht Kossel">Albrecht Kossel</a> after <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>ἀδήν</i> aden "gland", in reference to the pancreas, from which Kossel's sample had been extracted.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Experiments performed in 1961 by <a href="/wiki/Joan_Or%C3%B3" title="Joan Oró">Joan Oró</a> have shown that a large quantity of adenine can be synthesized from the <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> with five <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a> (HCN) molecules in aqueous solution;<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> whether this has implications for the <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Origin of life">origin of life</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> is under debate.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 8, 2011, a report, based on <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> studies with <a href="/wiki/Meteorites" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteorites">meteorites</a> found on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, was published suggesting building blocks of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> (adenine, <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a> and related <a href="/wiki/Organic_molecules" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic molecules">organic molecules</a>) may have been formed extraterrestrially in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Callahan_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Callahan-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Steigerwald_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steigerwald-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DNA_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 2011, physicists reported that adenine has an "unexpectedly variable range of ionization energies along its reaction pathways" which suggested that "understanding experimental data on how adenine survives exposure to <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultraviolet light">UV light</a> is much more complicated than previously thought"; these findings have implications for <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopic</a> measurements of <a href="/wiki/Heterocyclic" class="mw-redirect" title="Heterocyclic">heterocyclic</a> compounds, according to one report.<sup id="cite_ref-twsA35_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twsA35-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
